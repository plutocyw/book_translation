# Translation style guide

1. Use publication-quality Traditional Chinese and natural Taiwan usage; never use Simplified Chinese.
2. Use full-width Chinese punctuation in Chinese prose.
3. Preserve paragraph boundaries, dialogue, scene breaks, headings, emphasis when recoverable, and intentional ambiguity.
4. Translate meaning and voice rather than mechanically imitating English syntax.
5. Do not add lore explanations, summaries, euphemisms, or content absent from the source.
6. Use approved glossary and character-register forms exactly; do not confuse Western Kingdoms with Westmarch.
7. Keep `Den’Trag / Teeth of Trag’Oul` distinct from `Talon(s) of Trag’Oul`; they are different spells.
8. The ebook lost spaces around some emphasized words (`butknewit`, `Kentrilhad`, and similar forms). Recover the intended word boundary from syntax when unambiguous, but do not invent wording or silently skip malformed text.
9. Preserve colloquial contractions and character voice such as `nothin’`, `cap’n`, and `sittin’` with an equivalent natural register rather than literal apostrophe effects.
10. Format numbered chapter headings as H1 Markdown with Chinese numerals (`# 一` through `# 二十三`).
11. Use `呎／吋` for actual English measurements and `隻` as the Taiwan classifier glyph where appropriate.
